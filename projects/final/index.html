UMD’s Food Recovery Network: Fighting food insecurity one package at a time

By Natalie Davis

One in three Marylanders are living without reliable access to affordable healthy food, according to the Maryland Food Bank.

The Food Recovery Network, an organization that was founded at the University of Maryland in 2011, aims to distribute leftover food from campus dining halls to those in need in order to lessen the impact of food insecurity in College Park.

“I really like the purpose of it,” said senior Nathan Gallagher, a current Food Recovery Network shift leader. “I tried to get as involved as I possibly could,”

According to Gallagher, it is a great privilege to have the ability to take food that would otherwise be going to waste to the people who need it the most.

Currently, the Food Recovery Network has active chapters in 45 states and D.C

"It's a growing organization," Gallagher said of the Food Recovery Network.

Each week, Food Recovery Network shift leaders, like Gallagher, work to package the food from the dining halls to prepare it for transportation.

UMD's chapter alone has donated over 300,000 pounds of what otherwise would have been food waste since its beginning over a decade ago,with nearly a tenth of that recovered in the fall semester of 2016 alone, according to data from the Food Recovery Network.

However, the Food Recovery Network's shift leaders could not do this work without the help of volunteers.

“To me, it’s a shame that all this food would normally go to waste,” UMD junior and volunteer Jonathan Porrazzo said. “We’re able to recover it though, which is really awesome,”

Porrazzo expressed that volunteering is meaningful and rewarding to him because of the positive impact that he knows he is actively making.

Freshman Tracey Ho, another volunteer, expressed a similar sentiment.

“It is rewarding to know that I am giving back to others in that all of this food is going to go to someone who otherwise would not have it and who needs it a lot,” she said.

Sophomore Thomas Garda, a Food Recovery Network shift leader alongside Gallagher, said that he feels it is important to support the community around him.

“It’s very inspiring and rewarding to help out even if you are helping out just a small amount of people,” Garda said.

Together, Gallagher, Porrazzo, Ho, and Garda were able to package together nearly 162 pounds of food on April 18th to transport it to those in need.

All of the food on that day was recovered from one of the three dining halls on UMD's campus, 251 North. The most prevelant food item that day were vegetables, which had a recorded 28 pounds being recovered.

“It is shocking to see just the insanely large amount of food that we recover,” Garda said. “When I first came into this position I did not expect really nearly half as much as what we recover,”

“The food waste problem, like many other problems, spans the whole country and whole world and it is an incredibly massive issue, much larger than I feel like a lot of people come to realize,” he said, noting that it's an issue he feels should be further addressed.
